LOOTED ITEMS COLLECTION

Introduction

The LOOTED ITEMS COLLECTION was born with the aim of bringing together, for the first time, some of the world’s most iconic artefacts, linked by a dark history of colonialism and looting. As we move through time and space, this virtual experience shows the users how these treasures were created, what they meant to their original cultures, and the stories behind their removal during periods of conflict and colonial expansion. Each item on this tour offers a glimpse into the artistic, spiritual, and political life of civilizations long past, while also raising important questions about cultural heritage and restitution today. Whether the visitor is looking for a quick overview, more detailed context, or an in-depth analysis, this tour adapts to his preferences. The main purpose of the project is the creation of a platform which could work as an added value for a visit in the physical context as much as a complete virtual museum experience. The focus is all around the user which can decide to move inside the collection choosing the characteristics more compliant to make his needs. A personalized tour can be created by selecting and combining different sets of variables. The final result is a tour entirely created around the visitor objectives and needs.

Web Design Reference and Prototype

Visual Identity


We designed the visual identity for this project with a focus on creating a harmonious and aesthetically pleasing interface based on the golden ratio. The process involved the following steps:

Concept Development: We drew inspiration from classical Greek architecture, as reflected in the Parthenon-themed background images (e.g., partenone1.svg). This concept guided the overall design direction.

Color Palette: We defined a cohesive color scheme using CSS variables:
Primary Color: #B7B5E4
Secondary Color: #847979
Background Color: #0C0C0F
Accent Colors: #BBC2E2 and #CFCCD6

These colors ensure consistency and flexibility across the design.

Typography: We implemented a custom font, Karrik, using the @font-face rule. This font gives our design a unique character and complements the theme.

Layout Structure: Sections were strategically planned with heights like 100vh or 120vh to maintain a balanced layout. This approach ensures that content is well-organized and visually appealing.

Responsive Design: Media queries were utilized to adapt the design for various screen sizes, ensuring usability across devices, from desktops to mobile phones.

Design System Using CSS Variables

We built a scalable design system leveraging CSS variables, with proportions derived from the golden ratio (1.6181.618). This system ensures visual harmony and simplifies adjustments across typography, spacing, and layout.

Key Variables

--golden-ratio: 1.618;
        --base-font-size: 16px;
        --base-line-height: 1.5;
        --base-spacing: 1rem;

Typography Scaling

--spacing-small: calc(var(--base-spacing) / var(--golden-ratio));
            --spacing-large: calc(var(--base-spacing) * var(--golden-ratio));

By defining relationships between base values (e.g., font size, spacing), we ensured that all components scale proportionally. Adjusting any base variable automatically updates related elements throughout the design.

Tours

The Looted Items Collection offers a unique opportunity to personalize the exploration of the items. While personalization is key, three tours: Timeline, Colonial Conquests, and Geo represent the core ways to interact with the collection.

Narrative 1: Timeline

Focus: Historical and artistic value of the items

Description: Understanding the legacy of colonial looting and the cultural loss that accompanied it requires more than just looking at individual artefacts. When we arrange these looted items in chronological order, we uncover a broader, more complex story. One that shows the steady unfolding of imperial expansion, cultural encounters, and the lasting impact on societies around the world. This narrative was carefully constructed to guide the visitor through the timeline of CC events. Each artefact represents a moment in history when conflict, conquest, and imperial ambition collided with the cultural heritage of nations. By following this path, it will be possible to see how the forces of colonialism evolved over time, how different parts of the world were affected, and how these artefacts became symbols of both loss and resilience. From the oldest looted treasures to more recent historical conflicts, this journey highlights the changing dynamics of power and resistance, showing that the story of these items is also the story of the people and nations that continue to fight for their rightful return.

Narrative 2: Colonial Conquests and Cultural Loss

Focus: Social and political consequences of colonialism and the looting process.

Description: Colonialism profoundly altered the course of global history, not only through the conquest of territories but also through the systematic looting of cultural and historical treasures. This narrative delves into the darker legacy of colonialism, where artifacts, sacred objects, and symbols of national identity were forcibly removed from their homelands, stripping cultures of their heritage. The looted artifacts on display today serve as reminders of these violent encounters. This journey traces the stories behind the theft, the motivations of colonial powers, and the enduring impact on the communities who lost their cultural heritage. As we explore each artifact, we reflect on the ways colonial conquests shaped cultural identities and the ongoing debates surrounding restitution.

Narrative 3: Geo tour

Focus: Through the analysis of these items geographical collocation focus on the historical events related to each one of them.

Description: The GEO LOOTING TOUR invites visitors to explore the complex and often painful history of colonialism through the lens of cultural artifacts taken from their countries of origin. Each section highlights a specific continent, illuminating the stories behind the looted items that embody the rich cultural heritages of diverse societies. By understanding the historical context in which these treasures were appropriated, visitors can appreciate the significance of repatriation efforts and the ongoing conversations surrounding cultural ownership. As we journey through Africa, Asia, Europe, Oceania, and the Americas, we will uncover how colonial powers, motivated by curiosity, conquest, and economic gain, systematically stripped cultures of their historical artifacts, leaving lasting scars on the nations and communities affected.

Tour Personalization System

The tour selection is only the first variable in the visitor's tour personalization process. The looted items collection features a complex system of texts related to each item which adapt according to user decisions. Beyond tour selection, visitors can modify COMPLEXITY and LENGTH throughout their experience. Both of these variables move along a system of three different levels.

Complexity Levels

Fun: Simple and engaging language, emphasizing fun or key ideas. The use of technical terms is avoided but without compromising the informative and educational purpose of the experience. Ideal for a young audience, typically schools.

Basic: Expands with more context and specific information but still light in tone and easily understandable even without particular previous knowledge. Thought for visitors interested in the subject but without a related specific education.

Expert: Goes into historical details, source-based, with references to specific events, debates, or related scholarship.Provide texts ideal for academics or amateurs expert in the subject. A level of tour which can help these types of figures in their studies and maybe additionally enrich their knowledge.

Length Options

Short: General context, concise but informative.

Medium: Enhanced details and context, balanced presentation.

Long: Comprehensive narrative including dates, events, and implications.

By using and combining these variables texts change in order to reflect different lengths, competence levels, and tones. This structure is entirely controlled by the user by using special buttons which incorporate various commands like Tell me more, Tell me less, This text is too simple, This text is too difficult, or make the text more fun and enjoyable. Essentially the grid that forms at the end of the process is designed to accommodate diverse visitor types such as young audiences, generic adults, and scholars, and can easily be adapted for personalized use in the site.

Object page Visualization

Object page description and working logic

The object page is where the choices done previously by the user in the personalization tour unfold. Here there are the tree tours: Timeline Tour, Colonial Conquest Tour, and Geo Tour. In the timeline tour the objects are presented according to a chronological order (from the oldest to the newest); in the colonial conquest tour they are disposed in an alphabetical order depending on their colonial conquest period; while in the Geo Tour they follow an alphabetical order as well according to their country of origin, which also follows an alphabetical order.

Each tour starts with an image showing the object and with a text description immediately below. Depending on the previous choices, the initial text can be of different lengths and difficulties (ex: it can be short but complex or long but easy), and with the more/less buttons it is possible to adjust these two parameters according to one’s information needs. Above the image there are the buttons of the three tours so clicking on one of them is always possible to change tour and start from the beginning. It is also possible to start directly from the timeline tour by clicking on the “Start Tour” on the top right in the navbar and this in particular can be done in every page.

The object’s images, the text and their length and complexity are all managed thanks to a JavaScript code compose by different functions that deal with the fetch and display of the item’s images and texts according to a predefined order. All the functions are saved in the object-page.js file.
In this file there is first a getQueryParams() function that takes the query parameters from the current object page URL (e.g.?tour=Timeline%20Tour&length=short &complexity=fun) and it extracts the selected tour name (timeline, colonial conquest tour and geo tour), the preferred text length (short, medium, long), and text complexity (fun, basic, expert). This function actually exists for a potential use case. It is designed to extract parameters from the URL, which could be used to dynamically load content based on URL-based selections instead of relying solely on localStorage, so if the localStorage gets cleared, but the URL contains parameters, getQueryParams() could still provide content.

The next step is managed by the getItemsFromLocalStorage() function that access to the items data stored in the the localStorage. This is useful for checking if the items data exist: if it is not the case then the function returns null, otherwise it converts the Json string items data into a Javascript object and return it. This function is set as a global function by this line of code: window.getItemsFromLocalStorage = getItemsFromLocalStorage; this means that it can be used anywhere within the code.
But the real logic of the entire JavaScript file starts from the subsequent loadTourContent() function that retrieves the data stored in the browser’s localStorage under the key tourData. If no data is found, it returns null. The data in tourData key it has been saved previously in the localStorage and it contains all the choices made by the user in the personalize tour page. This means that there is another Javascript code (in our case the personalization.js file) who stores these choices in a const tourData and then save them using something like this: localStorage.setItem('tourData', JSON.stringify(tourData)). This function saves the data in the key tourData in a Json string format.

More specifically, in the personalization.js file there is the startTour() function that creates the const tourData with the user’s choices and save them in the localStorage. This function has been declared as global with the window.startTour = startTour, because it can be triggered outside the personalization.js file, for example just clicking the start button defined in the personalization_tour.html file and then it can redirects to the object-page.html with the user’s choices stored in the browser localStorage.

After being redirected to the object-page the loadTourContent function, as already mentioned, retrieves in the tourData key the choices saved in the localStorage in this way: const tourData = localStorage.getItem('tourData'). This retrieves the info contained in the tourData key saved as a string in the localStorage, then with the const { tourName, length, complexity } = JSON.parse(tourData); the string is parsed and converted into an object so it is possible to extract values and assign them to the variables tourName, length, complexity to be used later.

Then the loadTourContent function retrieves the content related to the variables tourName, length, complexity checking in a predefined dataset saved already inside the object-page.js file and accessible with the const data variable. This datased contains all the information to be shown on the object page. The title, the object images, the text with their complexity and length, the metadata information (Rdf graph) and the maps for the location of the object in the museum and in the world (with the original position and the actual position).

To show properly the objects a display function has been implemented which takes as parameters the length, the complexity and the index of the object to be displayed. Using something like this const itemId = items[index] and const itemData = texts[itemId] it is possible to retrieve the position of the item and its content in the dataset and then update dynamically with a document.getElementById() function each element of the object-page (title object, images, metadata, maps). For the object page navigation menu there is a dedicated code section where document.getElementById('prev'). addEventListener and document.getElementById('next').add EventListener deals with the scrolling from the previous to the next object and viceversa, while document.getElementById('length-less/length-more'). addEventListener in combination with changeLength('less') /changeLength('more') and changeComplexity('less')/ changeComplexity('more') functions manage the increase/decrease of text’s length/complexity of the current object.

In the Museum Localization and Geolocalization section there are maps for giving more information to the user about the locations of the object. The Museum Map shows the museum room where the object is placed, accompanied by a simple textual description of the room number; the second maps showing the original location and actual location in the world of the current displayed object. As well in this case there is a text simply explaining the previous and present location of the object.
Both Museum and Geolocalization Map have been inserted in the predefined dataset containing all the objects info and they are updated dynamically with a document.getElementById() function. The same goes for the textual references under the maps.

Metadata section

The last section of the Object page is dedicated to the metadata. This section called metadata-container is composed by two parts(metadata-content): the first one contains the Image of a Rdf Graph and a short text to guide the user to a better visualization of the graph itself; the second one is dedicated to a html table enriched with Dublin core Metadata elements.

Essentially, to guarantee a precise and detailed metadata representation for each item, it was decided to use the Dublin Core metadata elements as a vocabulary for a general description. Moreover, for a better understanding of the cultural and historical background as well as the item’s movement from one place to another, it was adopted the CIDOC CRM conceptual model in combination with other ontologies/models (such as DBpedia), and then created a RDF Graph to provide a complete visual representation.

Metadata Table

As already mentioned, a html table containing general Dublin Core metadata elements was created for each one of the 15 items and inserted within the metadata metadata-container section in a div container defined as metadata-content using a JavaScript code.

From the Dublin Core metadata were taken some of the main elements used when describing any resource, such as dc:title, dc:creator, dc:subject, dc:type, dc:description, dcterms:created, dcterms:spatial. The idea was to give general information about the author/s. date/place of creation, main subjects, title etc…

Metadata RDF Graph

Similar to what has been done with the Html Table, a RDF Graph was created to provide a different point of view about the historical and cultural background of the object, in particular whit a focus on the places where the object was created and/or found, moved from and to by foreign forces.

First of all, it was developed for each object a rdf file in Javascript, using CIDOC CRM conceptual model as reference, in combination with other models/vocabularies such as DBpedia, Wikidata, Geonames, XML Schema etc… On some occasion it was necessary to adopt a personalized ontology (items-ontology) created ad hoc for describing specific aspects of the item.

After that it was used the RDF grapher web service for parsing RDF data and visualizing it as a graph. What we got are high resolution images of the rdf graph to be put within the metadata-container. Collection Page

The Collection page serves as a comprehensive catalog of all artifacts in The Stolen Archive. It's designed to provide an accessible overview while maintaining deep integration with the rest of the site's features.

Key Features and Components

Grid Layout: Items are presented in a responsive grid format that adapts to different screen sizes, ensuring optimal viewing on all devices. Each item is displayed with high-quality imagery and basic identifying information.

Interactive Elements: Each artifact in the collection is clickable, leading visitors to the corresponding Map Page for deeper exploration of the item's journey and current location.

Tour Integration: Direct access to start any of the three main tours (Timeline, Colonial Conquests, Geo) from any item in the collection, creating seamless navigation between different viewing modes.

Map Page

The Map Page provides an interactive geographical interface that visualizes both the historical journey and current location of each artifact. This dual-map system offers visitors a comprehensive understanding of the object's spatial context.

Museum Map Features

Internal Navigation: Interactive floor plan showing the exact location of each artifact within the museum space.

Room Context: Detailed information about the exhibition space, including nearby artifacts and thematic connections.

Accessibility Information: Clear indicators for wheelchair access, rest areas, and facility locations.

World Map Features

Journey Visualization: Pop-ups showing the artifact's movement from its origin to its current location.

Historical Context: Timeline integration showing when and why each movement occurred.

Cultural Impact: Information about both the source community and current holding institution.

Tour Integration

The Map Page seamlessly integrates with all three main tours:

Timeline Tour Map: Chronological visualization of artifact movements, with dates and historical context.

Colonial Conquests Tour Map: Focus on colonial expansion patterns and their impact on cultural heritage.

Geo Tour Map: Emphasis on geographical relationships and cultural connections between regions.
