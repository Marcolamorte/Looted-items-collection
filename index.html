The Stolen Archive is a virtual exhibition which aims to explore the narratives surrounding artifacts that were appropriated during various eras of colonial history.

A Virtual Journey Through History's Stolen Treasures

Embark on a groundbreaking digital experience that sheds light on colonial history. The virtual tour takes you on an immersive journey through an extensive archive of cultural artifacts stolen during periods of colonization.

...but where were they looted from???

This systematic theft of cultural heritage began in earnest during the early colonial period (1500-1700) and reached its zenith during the imperial era (1700-1914).

The Benin Bronzes, forcibly seized in 1897, epitomize the violent nature of these acquisitions. Similarly, the removal of the Parthenon Marbles in the early 19th century and the looting of the Old Summer Palace in 1860 demonstrate the scale of cultural dispossession.

These acts were not merely about acquiring beautiful objects; they were deliberate attempts to assert dominance over colonized peoples by stripping them of their cultural symbols.

The ongoing debates surrounding repatriation, exemplified by cases like the Nok terracotta statues and the bust of Nefertiti, highlight the persistent impact of these historical injustices.

As we critically examine this history, it becomes clear that the restitution of these artifacts is not just about returning objects, but about addressing deep-seated inequalities.

Why This Matters

Our virtual tours aims to educate, provoke thought, and foster dialogue about the lasting impacts of colonialism on cultural heritage. By digitally reuniting these objects with their stories, we hope to contribute to the ongoing conversation about repatriation and cultural preservation.

Is a project developed for the final examination of the course Information Modelling and Web Technologies held by professor Fabio Vitali in the context of the Masters Degree program Digital Humanities and Digital Knowledge at Alma Mater Studiorum University of Bologna.
